Creating Algorithms Smart Enough to Predict Memory Performance Using EEG waves

9/17/18 9:55 PM   |   4 min read   |   Jump To: Overview

Overview

Under Dr. Michael Kahana at Penn's Computational Memory Lab, I have gotten comfortable with concepts like signal decomposition through Fourier analyses, band power extraction, supervised machine learning, nested cross-validation, and statistical significance tests. My research was most recently published in the Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition. Check out some of the stuff I've done!

The Variability Puzzle in Human Memory
Click here to download the full manuscript.

Memory performance exhibits a high level of variability from moment to moment. Much of this variability may reflect inadequately controlled experimental variables, such as word memorability, past practice and subject fatigue. Alternatively, stochastic variability in performance may largely reflect the effciency of endogenous neural processes that govern memory function. To help adjudicate between these competing views, we conducted a multisession study in which subjects completed 552 trials of a delayed free-recall task. Applying a statistical model to predict variability in each subject’s recall performance uncovered modest effects of word memorability, proactive interference, and other variables. In contrast to the limited explanatory power of these experimental variables, performance on the prior list strongly predicted current list recall. These findings suggest that endogenous factors underlying successful encoding and retrieval drive variability in performance.

What I'm Working On Now

I'm currently working on optimizing the parameters of a ridge regression that is trained to predict memory performance. My training data is composed of power values calculated from the EEG signals during the memory encoding portion of the experiment and the goal is to train a model to accurately predict retrieval from that data. The data I'm working with comes from the Penn Electrophysiology of Encoding and Retrieval Study, one of the largest-scale scalp EEG datasets in the world. Due to that, I have experience working with cluster computing and distributing jobs across worker nodes for large-scale analyses that would otherwise have taken days.

Startup Experience

Why I Took a Gap Year from Penn to Work Full-Time for a Startup

9/17/18 9:55 PM   |   6 min read   |   Jump To: Overview

Overview

After learning cross-platform app development in high school, I published an app on both the App Store and the Google Play Store that aimed to educate patients about upcoming medical procedures. At the end of my freshman year, a startup company called NeuroFlow emailed a listserv I was in looking for a mobile developer, and I spent the summer as a part-time intern building an app which supports real-time data streaming and integrates Bluetooth connections to biometric devices, and it worked well. NeuroFlow was in its exponential growth phase after seed money, and my internship turned into a full-time offer to join the company as the lead front-end engineer. I was also one of the first ten employees hired, so to say I got to learn a lot would be a huge understatement.

What is NeuroFlow?
Click here to visit the marketing website I made for NeuroFlow.

NeuroFlow’s HIPAA-compliant, cloud-based platform empowers health care providers to objectively assess, track, and engage patients using evidence-based practices to support their mental health.

My Work
Click here to visit the NeuroFlow platform.

Being the only front-end developer at NeuroFlow, I got to design, code, and maintain the entire front-end UI/UX codebase for our software platform by myself for over a year. I learned and employed Material design principles with the constant goal of designing something complex and robust but making it as user-friendly for non-technical users as possible. My work also included parsing and displaying large amounts of data in understandable and user-friendly ways, which was always a challenge I enjoyed. Our development team used the Agile methodology with scrum techniques, and highly emphasized best practices such as detailed reviewing of pull requests and up-to-date Jira boards.

I also created and maintained the marketing website for NeuroFlow, which I chose to do through WordPress. I was responsible for the domain and hosting for this site, including SSL certificate purchasing, domain transfers and forwarding, and emergency response to host-side errors. I set up Google Analytics on both the software website and the marketing website, and used tools like Hotjar and Google Tag Manager for customer segmentation and click-flow tracking. I got comfortable enough with Adobe Photoshop to design flowcharts and mockups for the site, and even got to do some video editing after setting up the company's YouTube channel.
